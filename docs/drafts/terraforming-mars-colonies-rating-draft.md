# Terraforming Mars: Colonies Expansion Rating Draft

Status: public bilingual page drafted at `public/rating/boardgame/expansions/terraforming-mars/colonies.html`.

## Provisional Review Scope

This draft evaluates the original 2018 `Colonies` expansion. Later promotional cards and cross-expansion content are not automatically included.

The official box introduces:

- colony tiles, with the number in play generally determined by player count;
- a `17 M€` standard project for building a colony;
- a new trade action, paid with `9 M€`, `3 energy`, or `3 titanium`;
- colony placement bonuses, trade income, and colony-owner bonuses;
- colony production during the Solar Phase;
- 49 project cards, 5 corporations, and 11 colony tiles.

These facts define the review object. They do not require every component to receive equal space in the article.

## Already Resolved

- `Colonies` adds a powerful strategic route with a large effect on each session.
- Its central system connects more tightly to the base economy than `Venus Next`: trading behaves like an important additional action inside the existing game rather than a parallel track attached above it.
- Its most distinctive resource contribution is not a repair or revaluation of the existing energy model. `Colonies` adds a new three-energy threshold model.
- The plant threshold is primarily a resource threshold: players can accumulate eight plants through production, cards, and other gains. Energy normally converts into heat during production instead of remaining stored across generations, so the three-energy trade threshold is reached mainly by raising energy production itself. The expansion therefore moves Terraforming Mars's familiar phase-change design from stored resources into production capacity.
- This new threshold integrates with the existing energy-to-heat conversion instead of bypassing it. Energy production now supports an immediate trade opportunity before its resources later become heat, making the relationship among production, energy, heat, and trade more dimensional.
- With a strong colony available, three energy production can approach nine money production. Requiring three energy keeps the conversion from becoming freely available, while trade-cost reductions offer alternative ways to cross or lower the threshold.
- The new action and the new energy threshold are related but not identical merits. The article should explain both instead of treating the whole expansion as an energy patch.
- Different colony tiles do not create player imbalance merely because some are stronger than others. The same public setup is available to everyone, so a strong tile can change the session without making the opportunity inherently unfair.
- High trade income is likewise not a deduction by itself. Shared power inflation matters only if it destroys meaningful choices or grants unequal access; everyone becoming stronger is not automatically a design problem.
- Building colonies can create a more concrete turn-order issue because each tile has only three colony spaces. A later player may lose access to a highly desirable tile. This is an access and seating-order question, not evidence that the public colony tile itself is unbalanced.
- Trade order is less concerning. Acting first does not make an immediate trade automatically efficient, and the first-player position rotates.
- The expansion both accelerates and slows different parts of play. Greater resource throughput can speed development, while additional engine opportunities and trade decisions can extend it. The overall effect on game length is approximately neutral rather than a clear acceleration or delay.
- The shared economy is a central strength. Colony owners want other players to trade at their tile and trigger owner bonuses, while every visitor competes for timing and consumes the accumulated trade value. This produces meaningful competition and mutual benefit inside the same action.

## Confirmed Calibration

- Quality tier: `S / 卓越扩展` (confirmed).
- Enable recommendation: `Usually include / 通常加入` (retained from the current calibration; suitable after players understand the base game, then normally left in).
- Reason for `S`: the expansion adds a consequential new system, integrates it into the existing economy, creates another area of competition, and extends the resource model through a three-energy trade threshold whose phase change falls mainly on energy production. Its added rules and recurring upkeep clearly earn their place, so it fully meets and may exceed the broad S-tier description.
- Why this is not `S+`: unlike `Prelude`, it does not create its improvement at almost zero cost. Limited colony spaces can create some turn-order friction, and a few added cards and corporations can bend the access rules or numerical balance. These are real but bounded issues: they separate `S` from `S+` without pulling the expansion into `A`.
- Why this is not `Always include / 常驻`: the expansion is unsuitable for a first teaching game, but only to a moderate degree; it remains entirely playable for newcomers. Colony construction, three trade payment options, accumulated income, owner bonuses, fleet occupancy, and colony recovery add a valuation layer before new players have established the base game's resource values and timing as a comparison standard. They can use the system, but cannot yet judge its opportunity costs or appreciate what it changes as clearly. This is an enable question, not a deduction from its quality tier.

## Codex Answers Awaiting User Review

### Building Versus Trading

The `17 M€` standard project creates a meaningful investment decision because a player does not need a colony in order to trade. Paying for the colony buys an immediate placement bonus and a claim on future colony bonuses, especially when other players keep visiting that tile. Declining to build preserves money while still allowing access to the public trade income. A strong tile can make early investment attractive, but that does not make colony construction universally compulsory; the important friction is whether its three spaces remain available long enough for every interested player.

The system also mixes competition and mutual benefit. Owners want a colony to remain attractive enough that other players trade there, while every trade consumes that tile's accumulated value for the generation. The timing contest is therefore useful even when the trade itself is generous.

### Added Cards And Corporations

The project cards are generally well integrated because they alter existing costs, tags, resource production, colony placement, and trade actions rather than creating a second isolated engine. The clearest balance concern lies in the rare cards that break the system's two scarcity rules: extra trade fleets allow more than one trade per generation, while `Research Colony` and `Space Port Colony` can let one player occupy more than one space on the same tile. These effects can be much stronger than an ordinary trade discount because they change access rather than merely improving efficiency.

The five corporations are more uneven than the shared colony system. `Poseidon` scales with colony construction and therefore with player count; `Aridor` gains flexibility from the available tag pool; `Polyphemos` and `Stormcraft` are much more conditional, and the base-game discussion already identified `Stormcraft` as a corporation whose numerical model is difficult to justify. This looks like a contained content-balance deduction rather than a failure of the central colony mechanism.

### Upkeep And Learning Cost

The recurring administration is real but compact: fleets return and each colony marker advances once per generation. Experienced players can resolve this quickly, and the benefit of the system earns that upkeep. New players still have to learn colony construction, three trade currencies, trade income, owner bonuses, fleet occupancy, and marker recovery. That is enough to keep the expansion out of a first teaching game even though it does not make veteran sessions cumbersome.

### Player Count

The official setup scales the available tiles with player count, which protects strategic variety, but it cannot make the table dynamics identical. With fewer players, there are fewer trades and colony owners receive fewer bonuses triggered by opponents. With more players, colonies fill faster, ownership pays more often, and competition for both spaces and trade timing becomes sharper. The expansion should remain functional across counts, but its shared-economy and interaction strengths are likely most visible in multiplayer rather than solo or a low-interaction two-player session.

### Current Calibration

These answers support the confirmed `S` quality judgment and the separate `Usually include` recommendation. The shared strength of colonies and trade does not pull the grade down, while non-zero upkeep and teaching cost are repaid by the expansion's design value. Limited-space turn-order friction and a small number of cards and corporations that bend the expansion's access rules or numerical balance separate the box from `S+`, but do not materially limit its overall achievement.

## Terminology Note

- `月冥` is provisionally understood as shorthand for the Luna and Pluto colony tiles. The public page does not depend on that interpretation: it refers generally to desirable colony tiles and limited spaces.
