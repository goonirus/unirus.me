# Terraforming Mars: Turmoil Expansion Rating Draft

Status: public bilingual page drafted at `public/rating/boardgame/expansions/terraforming-mars/turmoil.html`.

## Review Scope

This draft evaluates the original 2019 `Turmoil` expansion. The separate Turmoil Kickstarter Promo Pack, later promotional cards, and cross-expansion content are not included in the tier.

The original expansion introduces:

- the Terraforming Committee and six political parties;
- player and neutral delegates, party leadership, the chairman, dominance, and influence;
- one free lobby delegate per player and generation, with additional delegates costing `5 M€`;
- 31 Global Event cards, first shown three generations before resolution;
- a ruling bonus and active policy for each party;
- a recurring Turmoil phase, including the loss of `1 TR` per player and generation;
- 16 project cards and 5 corporations.

## Confirmed Analysis

### The Central Value

- The committee creates a recurring public contest in a game whose interaction is otherwise often fine-grained and indirect.
- The six parties are strongly integrated with the base economy. Their policies and bonuses change the value of tags, production, titanium, greenery placement, tile placement, card draw, and TR timing.
- Global Events are shown three generations before resolution. This converts surprise output randomness into partial forecasting and adaptation.
- Neutral delegates connect the future-event queue to the current political map, while influence gives political participation consequences beyond winning the next ruling party.
- The political and social theme is a natural extension of a growing Martian civilization and gives the expansion strong thematic coherence.

### The Burden Is Structural

- The added burden is paid every generation: lobbying decisions, delegate majorities, party leadership, policy changes, ruling bonuses, Global Event resolution, neutral-delegate movement, lobby refill, and TR revision.
- This is not merely a longer rules explanation. The expansion repeatedly asks the table to redirect attention from its card engines to a second public optimization layer.
- One free delegate keeps politics from becoming a system only wealthy players can enter. Additional delegates at `5 M€` create a real opportunity cost. The model works, but open majorities also encourage late bidding and turn-order-sensitive sniping.
- Losing `1 TR` each generation is a clear way to pay for committee bonuses, but it is blunt. Together with additional actions and resolution steps, it lengthens an already long game.
- The expansion's return is conditional on an experienced group that values public politics and has enough time. Its recurring cost is not conditional.

### Variation Is Not Replayability

- Event order changes short-term values and timing, but this is mainly tactical variation rather than a large new family of long-term strategic routes.
- The six parties are fixed and become increasingly legible with experience.
- The base game already has a very large card and corporation space. `Turmoil` does not add enough meaningful strategic variety to treat replayability as one of its central achievements.
- Future visibility improves planning, but visibility does not turn every random input into agency.

### Player Count

- Three- and four-player games best support competing interests, temporary alignment, collective denial, and meaningful political control.
- Two-player games lose much of that multi-interest political structure and tend toward binary counter-bidding.
- Neutral delegates occupy a much larger share of the political system at two players. Because the random Global Event order determines where they enter, random input can shape party dominance more strongly than the players' political choices.
- Paying additional money to correct a randomly preloaded parliament is not the same as gaining meaningful replayability. In two-player games it can feel like paying a tax to recover agency.
- The full recurring administration does not shrink with player count, so two-player sessions receive less political value for nearly the same procedural burden.
- Public recommendation boundary: `Turmoil` is normally not recommended at two players.

### Secondary Content

- The 16 project cards use party requirements, delegates, influence, and existing base-game resources to connect the committee with the main deck rather than creating an isolated card family.
- The five corporations are less consistent in balance and configuration dependence than the central political system. This is a secondary deduction rather than the main reason for the tier.
- Later promotional and cross-expansion cards are outside the scored scope.

## Final Calibration

- Quality tier: `B / 有得有失`.
- Enable recommendation: `Include as needed / 按需加入`.
- Why `B` rather than `A`: the political layer has real, coherent, and well-integrated design value, but its recurring administration, longer play time, attention cost, two-player scaling problem, and partly noisy event influence materially shape every session. The experience returned is not large enough to say that the burden is fully justified.
- Why not `B+`: the expansion is not close to the A-tier requirement that added burden be fully repaid. Its central weakness is the efficiency of the whole module, not a handful of peripheral flaws.
- Why not `C`: in experienced three- or four-player groups that actively want more public interaction, the committee and event forecasting can meaningfully improve the game. The system functions and integrates; it is overbuilt, not a failed or clearly detrimental expansion.
- Enable boundary: skip it for teaching games, normally skip it at two players, and use it at three or four players only when the group wants a longer and more political version of `Terraforming Mars`.
