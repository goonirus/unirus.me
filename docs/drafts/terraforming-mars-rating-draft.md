### 已确认核心判断

《殖民火星》在你的评价里是“纯粹而极致的引擎构筑”。印象分在 `99-100/100` 之间，不是因为它没有缺点，而是因为你确实非常喜欢它所提供的完整引擎体验。

这个判断负责为文章定调，但不是一条要求所有章节服从的“主线”。后续仍然按资源、卡牌、时代结构、公共版图、企业、里程碑与奖项、扩展等具体对象逐项讨论；大纲只负责防止遗漏，不限制论证必须沿一条线展开。

这里的“防止遗漏”指防止漏掉重要判断，不是要求穷举全部规则。只有能够形成独立观点、影响评分或推荐边界、或者本身特别值得分析的机制才进入正文。无限手牌已经用于说明《殖民火星》的长期规划，不再作为新的待讨论项重复展开。

### 评价对象与版本边界

### 已确认的结构判断

《殖民火星》的核心不只是“牌多”，而是把庞大牌库放进了一套长周期结构：一个时代没有严格的行动次数上限，也没有硬性的手牌上限。玩家可以藏牌、积累资源、搭建引擎，再决定何时把积累转化成分数。因此，它更偏向资源积累与长期规划，而不是短循环里的每回合转化效率。

### 已经说清楚的内容

1. **卡牌结构**：绿卡偏即时收益，蓝卡偏长期收益；标记与前置需求共同支撑引擎构筑。
2. **数值模型**：大多数卡牌处于稳定区间，模型精密而且延展性很强；扩展和 promo 通常能继续开发原有系统，电资源也借《殖民地》获得了新的位置。
3. **明确例外**：仍有少数特别超模或特别弱的牌，《地下爆破》是明确举过的弱牌例子。
4. **长期规划**：火星高手可以藏牌，在最后一个时代完成引擎并集中爆分；这是你本人也偏好的玩法。
5. **卡牌投资**：“反重力引擎”即使最后打不出，名义损失也只有两块，但必须保留“前期三块和后期一块不能直接相减”的时间价值限定。
6. **美术**：卡牌 UI 是明确扣分项；插图也没有动物照片那么形象可亲，但这还不足以代表整个美术维度。

### 创新性：机制溯源与评分口径

- 机制溯源与创新性评分不是一回事。溯源用于辨认哪些工具已有前例；评分要判断《殖民火星》在自己真正创新的范围内是否形成了鲜明、重要而且完成度高的特色。
- 卡牌、资源、轮抽、六边形铺设、非对称能力和轮流行动等基础机制广泛存在于其他游戏中。它们不是《殖民火星》的原创，但也不能仅仅因为“有前例”就成为创新性扣分项。
- 最值得计入创新性的，是公共改造参数、TR、卡牌环境门槛、个人经济成长和游戏结束时机组成的完整循环。玩家既建设私人引擎，又能通过改造火星控制这些引擎还能运行多久；这一结构直接改变了整局游戏的决策方式。
- 卡牌的“先支付研发费用保留项目、以后再支付完整实施费用”不是完全没有前例的两阶段投资，却在这里成为经济系统的中心，并通过项目与专利的主题获得了清楚的解释。
- 里程碑的“最先达成”和奖励的“最终最多”都有更早的目标机制可供比较；真正特别的是玩家需要花钱开启一个自己未必能赢得的奖励类别。
- 全扩展与 Promo 的价值还包括系统架构上的可延展性。各模块使用的基础机制未必新，但能继续接入原有卡牌、标签、资源和公共参数，是创新完成度的一部分。

### Depth/Complex：91/100（当前结论）

《殖民火星》的精通门槛并不来自复杂的基础规则。它的回合结构和行动方式都很简单；牌库虽然庞大，却使用了相当统一、清晰的卡牌语言。有卡牌游戏经验的玩家通常很容易理解每张牌在做什么。因此，这里的 Complexity 主要来自熟悉大量卡牌的内容成本，而不是理解规则的结构成本。

真正的难点在于发现并掌握数值模型。没有掌握模型时，玩家即使积累了许多对局，也可能仍然只能凭感觉判断一张牌强不强；一旦理解钱产、资源、标签、时机和回合价值之间的换算，即使游玩局数不多，也可以开始计算一张牌究竟超模还是亏模，许多选择会迅速变得清晰。自行摸索这套模型非常困难，除非有人直接讲解，因此从自行探索的角度看，它的精通门槛不低，深度也绝不浅。

但同一套模型也是深度继续上升时的明确瓶颈。掌握模型以后，许多卡牌判断会从经验问题转化为相对清晰的计算问题，游戏不会因此无限打开新的理解层次。因此，`91/100` 评价的不是极高的绝对深度，而是《殖民火星》用很低的规则复杂度承载了扎实的数值判断、长期规划和时机选择。这个分数必须同时看 Depth 与 Complexity，不能只按照精通上限判断。

### Flow：90/100（当前结论）

《殖民火星》的新手局和熟练局有明显区别。新手需要花较长时间读牌，但这是整桌共同经历的学习过程，而且卡牌本身并不晦涩。费用、标签、图标和数值拥有很强的表意性，大多数卡牌不必依赖大段文字才能理解；有卡牌游戏经验的玩家尤其容易读懂这套语言。

熟练以后，这种信息设计的优势会变得更加明显。老手扫一眼就能判断一张牌的用途，即使第一次接触新扩展，也通常只需看过一遍便能大致判断卡牌的水平。全扩展增加的阶段维护和后期引擎执行在熟练后基本不会打断流程，因此一局虽然可能很长，实际游玩仍然十分流畅。游戏时长与 Flow 需要分开判断，长并不自动等于拖沓。

评分不能完全按照老手标准来看待。新手局仍然会因为读牌、比较和重新规划而出现真实停顿，扩展过多时更加明显，其中 Turmoil 尤其折磨。因此，强表意性与熟练局的顺畅支持高分，却不足以让它进入顶级 Flow。当前结论调整为 `90/100`。推荐边界仍然成立：新玩家应从较少模块开始，而不是把完整全扩版本作为第一次接触游戏的默认配置。

## Core Judgment, Not A Mandatory Thesis

The confirmed core judgment is that `Terraforming Mars` is engine building in an unusually pure and extreme form. This orients the verdict, but it must not become a compulsory thesis that forces every mechanism, dimension, and caveat into one linear argument.

One already established mechanism argument is not simply that `Terraforming Mars` has a large deck. It is that the deck sits inside a long-horizon time structure:

- A generation has no comparable hard cap on the number of actions a player may take before passing.
- There is no strict hand limit forcing immediate disposal of long-term possibilities.
- Players can hold cards, accumulate resources, build an engine, and choose when to convert that engine into points.
- The game therefore emphasizes resource accumulation and long-term planning more strongly than short-cycle conversion efficiency.

This argument is well supported by the earlier comparison. It is one major explanation of the engine-building experience, not a required lead for every section.

## Confirmed Material

### 1. Card Language And Engine Roles

- Green cards primarily provide immediate returns.
- Blue cards primarily provide persistent or long-term value.
- Tags and prerequisites are central to card access and engine construction.
- Awards are a useful comparison point for player-introduced scoring objectives, but Awards themselves have not yet received a full evaluation.
- The deck is not just large; its cards operate inside a coherent resource and tag model.

### 2. Time Structure And Long-Term Planning

- Turn structure is more fundamental than card similarity when comparing `Terraforming Mars` with `Ark Nova`.
- A generation can become extremely long because action count is not fixed in the same way as a short-cycle game.
- Players compete more directly through accumulated resources.
- The lack of a strict hand limit supports long-term planning and card retention.
- Skilled players may hold cards, finish an engine late, and create an explosive final generation.
- The user identifies personally with this long-planning, card-holding play style.

### 3. Numerical Model And Extensibility

- The tag and value model is intricate and generally well tuned.
- Most cards remain within a stable value range.
- The model is highly extensible: expansions and promotional cards have generally improved or developed the system rather than merely adding volume.
- `Colonies` extends the numerical model by adding a three-energy trading threshold. Because energy normally converts into heat instead of remaining stored across generations, this threshold is reached mainly through energy production rather than gradual resource accumulation.
- A few highly overpowered cards and a few exceptionally weak cards still exist.
- `Underground Detonations` is the user's concrete example of an exceptionally weak card.

### 4. Card Investment And Timing

- Buying `Anti-Gravity Technology` early is a precise example of how card investment works.
- If the card is eventually played, its strength can largely repay the investment.
- If it is never played, the player can still recover one money by selling the patent, so the nominal loss is two money.
- Preserve the timing caveat: three money early and one money late cannot be treated as a simple, context-free difference of two.

### 5. Aesthetics Already Stated

- The card UI is a real weakness.
- The illustrations are less immediate and approachable than `Ark Nova`'s animal photography.
- This supports a negative judgment of the card presentation, but it does not yet establish the complete Aesthetics score for boards, tiles, components, or overall table presence.

### 6. Innovation: Provenance Versus Rating

- Historical provenance and the Innovation score answer different questions. A precedent identifies an established tool; it is not automatically a deduction.
- Common foundations such as cards, resources, drafting, hex placement, variable powers, and alternating actions should not lower the score merely because they predate `Terraforming Mars`.
- The central innovation candidate is the integrated loop connecting shared terraforming parameters, TR, card requirements, private economic growth, and player-controlled game length.
- The research-and-implementation payment model and player-funded Awards are distinctive implementations even though related two-stage investments and first/most goals have earlier examples.
- Expansion modularity and compatibility are relevant to the completion of the innovation, even when an individual expansion uses an established mechanism.
- Confirmed Innovation score: `87/100`. Existing precedents are not automatic deductions; the moderate-high result reflects that the game's main achievement lies more in integration, modelling, and extensibility than in a large number of unprecedented mechanisms.
- A similar earlier structure is evidence of precedent, not evidence that Jacob Fryxelius borrowed it. No direct influence should be claimed without a reliable source.

### 7. Resource And Production Model

- Money, steel, and titanium have clear, intuitive divisions as spending resources. Steel and titanium carry more value per unit but are less flexible than money because their use is restricted by card tags.
- Their production values are not simple conversions of the printed spending rates. The user's card-model estimates are approximately `4.5 M€` for one money production, `7 M€` for one steel production, and `10 M€` for one titanium production.
- Those model values broadly match the user's play experience. The restricted-payment rule and the numerical model support each other rather than operating as unrelated systems.
- Plant resources are strong because greenery is strong, but the conversion occurs only after reaching eight plants. Cards that remove plants can interrupt that threshold, creating meaningful interaction and timing play.
- Energy is awkward in the base game. It is priced above heat because energy production also satisfies many city requirements and powers valuable cards such as `AI Central`, yet its default conversion into the comparatively weak heat resource leaves it expensive without being broadly useful.
- `Colonies` does not simply repair or reprice energy. It adds a new three-energy trading model. Plants undergo a resource-level phase change after players assemble eight resources through production, cards, and other gains; energy normally cannot be stored across generations because it converts into heat, so its three-energy phase change is reached mainly by raising production itself.
- This is a further mechanism-level development of Terraforming Mars's threshold design. The new use remains connected to the old energy-to-heat conversion: energy production creates an immediate trading opportunity and then retains its delayed relationship with heat, making the model more dimensional rather than replacing it.
- When the available colony is strong, three energy production can function close to nine money production. Requiring three energy prevents this outlet from becoming automatically excessive, while cards that reduce trading costs create additional routes into or below the threshold.
- Heat production itself remains weak. Preserve the user's concrete comparison: paying `35 M€` for `Soletta` can require three to four generations before it produces enough heat for three temperature increases, whereas `Deimos Down` costs `31 M€`, raises temperature three steps immediately, provides four steel, and can remove the equivalent of an entire greenery in plants.
- Heat production is also strategically misaligned with terraforming strategies that want to end the game quickly, because production needs later generations to repay its investment.
- `Terraforming Mars` has no short axis comparable to `Ark Nova`; its long-horizon engine structure is better suited to players who are good at developing an economy over time.
- Because a later card may reduce costs, a card that provides no long-term return can theoretically be delayed until the final generation. This is not an absolute rule: playing it earlier may cycle into another card, create a chance at a useful draw, or secure a Milestone.
- Milestones are therefore an important restraint on postponing everything without further thought.
- TR simultaneously supplies income and points. That dual role supports a fast-terraforming strategy in which early terraforming helps finance further terraforming and creates a snowball of immediate conversion.
- This produces a conflict over game length. Engine-focused players want more generations: they obtain production more cheaply than TR early, then use the resulting late-game resources to score more heavily. Terraforming-focused players want to convert quickly and end the game sooner.
- The conflict comes directly from the end condition. The game does not end after a fixed number of generations or when someone reaches a score threshold; it ends when the shared, apparently cooperative objective of completing the terraforming has been met.
- The competition between these approaches is usually most visible in the final two generations.
- TR and the wider numerical model keep the development and terraforming approaches broadly balanced. The boundary is deliberately not absolute, leaving room for players to mix terraforming with controlled delay.
- Keep this argument in the mechanism discussion. The later Balance section should refer back to it instead of relocating or duplicating the full explanation.

### 8. Global Parameters

- Reducing another player's production or resources belongs in the later Interactivity discussion. Do not pull that material forward into the resource-and-production section.
- Apart from combinations produced by particular corporations, the three global parameters do not generally impose distinct strategic identities or restrict players to one terraforming route. The corporation observation is marked **non-public** and should not enter the article from this answer.
- The main relationship among temperature, oxygen, and oceans comes from card prerequisites and rewards printed along the tracks.
- Several unusually strong plant cards require enough oceans or temperature, while some strong ocean cards also require temperature.
- The user currently sees this as a distinctive feature without a clear independent advantage or disadvantage. Do not turn it into a broad claim that the entire game is balanced merely because strong cards have requirements.
- **Working interpretation, not yet confirmed:** the requirements place projects into different stages of the changing Martian environment, giving the large deck a shared temporal structure. This explains what the system does without forcing an unsupported claim about overall balance.
- The parameter tracks also reward progress in one parameter by advancing another: an oxygen threshold advances temperature, and a temperature threshold adds an ocean.
- These cross-track rewards are valuable enough to create natural points of competition. Even an engine-focused player who ordinarily wants to prolong the game may use an otherwise highly inefficient Standard Project to claim the parameter reward first.
- The importance of the reward is therefore not just its amount. Near a threshold, it can temporarily change a player's position in the wider conflict over when the game should end.

### 9. Map Placement

- The two-money ocean adjacency bonus is only an additional benefit. It introduces some spatial planning, but it is not the main source of map play.
- City and greenery placement restrictions create the real positional contest.
- Cities are expensive and have extremely high scoring variance. A poorly placed city can score nothing from adjacent greeneries, while the geometric ceiling is six points; the user regards fully realizing that ceiling as practically unattainable in a real game.
- Because cities cannot be placed next to other cities, placing one can also deny valuable city positions to another player.
- Greenery placement is constrained by the player's existing tiles. In public prose, state the complete rule accurately: if possible, a greenery must be placed next to another tile owned by that player; only when no legal adjacent area exists may it be placed elsewhere.
- This makes route planning matter. Without preparing an appropriate chain of owned tiles, a player may be forced to place greenery beside an opponent's city and give that opponent points.
- The map contest therefore comes from the different spatial restrictions and incentives of cities and greeneries, not primarily from the ocean adjacency payment.
- Map placement should be evaluated through the incentives it creates for players generally, not through a narrow question about what the user personally would do in one position.
- As a general map-design pattern, highly profitable placement spaces tend to be surrounded by areas with sparse placement resources, limiting how easily a player can continue developing from the initial gain.
- Some maps include spaces that are especially profitable for engine-focused play, such as the three-card placement reward.
- Claiming such a reward with greenery forces an engine-focused player to advance oxygen and therefore move the shared game toward its end.
- A terraforming-focused player must also consider whether to claim it: doing so gains part of the economic reward and denies it to an opponent, but may constrain that player's own future tile route.
- Maps also use Milestones and Awards to compensate otherwise barren spaces or regions, giving players a reason to develop areas that would receive little attention from placement bonuses alone.

### 11. Milestones And Awards

- Interpret the raw opening phrase `里程碑的奖项` as `里程碑和奖项`: the answer immediately analyzes the two systems separately. Preserve the raw wording above, but use the clear intended conjunction in structured and public prose.
- Milestones offer an unusually favorable direct return, but qualifying for one often requires actions or investments that would not otherwise be efficient.
- The resulting contest is not only a calculation of the user's own progress. Players must judge whether opponents will invest in Milestones and which specific Milestones each opponent is likely to pursue.
- That uncertainty makes failed investment the real punishment. Taking inefficient steps toward a Milestone and then losing the race can leave the player with little compensation.
- Awards deepen the same kind of contest by adding a funding-timing decision. Funding early is cheaper, while the third Award costs `20 M€` and may offer little expected profit relative to its price.
- Early funding carries much greater risk because the category remains open for opponents to contest. The player who pays to fund an Award may ultimately create points for someone else.

### 12. Expansion Mechanisms In The Complete Game

- `Prelude` compresses the slow opening buildup and accelerates the strategic tempo of the game. Its additional opening variation also improves replayability. The main review does not need card-by-card analysis.
- The primary contribution of `Venus Next` is not simply another way to gain TR. Its large set of Venus cards introduces new numerical models, mechanisms, synergies, and therefore new strategic routes.
- Its special TR route for engine-focused players and the appealing “Venus animals” are secondary benefits.
- The most interesting `Venus Next` mechanism is World Government Terraforming. It keeps the game progressing while also making threshold rewards on the global-parameter tracks more fiercely contested.
- `Colonies` likewise creates a powerful new strategic route with a large effect on each session. It connects more tightly to the base economy than `Venus Next`: Venus can feel like a module attached above the base game, whereas Colonies adds an important action inside the existing system.
- A major contribution of `Colonies` is its new three-energy threshold model. Because the threshold is normally reached through production rather than stored energy, it extends the base game's phase-change design and connects trade to the existing energy-to-heat cycle. The fuller resource-model argument already appears in the resource section and should not be repeated here.
- `Turmoil` is controversial because it partly changes the game's positioning and audience. Its political layer is coherent and connects strongly to existing tags, resources, production, tile placement, TR, and card timing; the parliament gives players a recurring public contest rather than an isolated side game.
- That value comes with a large module-wide cost. Repeated political administration, the recurring TR reduction, longer play time, and additional attention load make the expansion substantially bulkier than the experience it reliably returns.
- Global Events are visible three generations ahead and therefore create useful forecasting, but changing event order mostly adds tactical variation rather than a major new family of long-term routes. This is not one of the expansion's central replayability strengths.
- The scaling problem is clearest at two players: politics becomes more binary, neutral delegates and random event order exert proportionally more influence, and nearly the full administrative burden remains. The detailed expansion verdict is therefore `B / Include as needed`, aimed mainly at experienced three- or four-player groups that actively want a longer and more political game.
- `Prelude 2` does not need separate analysis in the main review because its role is broadly continuous with `Prelude`. Additional maps and similar content can receive one or two sentences noting the extra variation and replayability.
- These concise conclusions are sufficient for the main review. Detailed expansion evaluation belongs on the planned individual expansion pages.
