Making the Ball Bounce,
the Way It Should

Donglin Yan • University of Gothenburg

1. What I Worked On

Halfway through the course feels like a good moment to pause and look at what I’ve actually been doing. So far, most of my time has gone into three things: a Tapper prototype in pure C# (no engine), updating parts of the engine from SDL2 to SDL3, and fixing collision response that looked “almost right” but wasn’t.

These weren’t part of some grand connected plan — just different exercises and tasks across the course — but they each forced me to pay attention to different layers of “stuff engines normally hide.”

2. Tapper (No Engine)

The Tapper exercise was basically a controlled way to experience what game development feels like without an engine: only standard .NET, no external libraries, no Unity, and “ASCII is fine.” I made a very simple console version, and it did what it needed to do.

2.1 The Friction

The gameplay logic wasn’t the hard part. The hard part was that everything around it became my responsibility. Collision checks became nested loops. Object removal became “iterate backwards or crash.” Rendering was clearing the console and redrawing, which is functional but not exactly pleasant.

The experience felt less like “designing a game” and more like “keeping the loop alive.” Not because C# is bad — it’s because without engine-level structure, the boring bookkeeping grows fast.

2.2 What Stuck With Me

I wouldn’t say Tapper taught me C#. I already knew enough C# to finish it. What it gave me was a very concrete sense of why engines exist at all. They’re not only about fancy graphics — they’re about removing a huge amount of repetitive, error-prone maintenance so you can think at the level you actually care about.

The biggest lesson from Tapper wasn’t “this is impossible without an engine.” It was: “this is possible, but you will spend your limited time fighting infrastructure instead of making choices.”

3. SDL2 → SDL3

Later, I worked on updating parts of the course engine from SDL2 to SDL3. I originally assumed it would mostly be “replace old API calls with new ones.” It was not that simple.

3.1 Small Changes Aren’t Small

Some changes compiled immediately but behaved differently at runtime — especially around initialization and audio device handling. That was the annoying part: there wasn’t always a clean “error message” telling me what I did wrong. The only way forward was testing, isolating changes, and being careful not to touch too many things at once.

3.2 A Slower Workflow

This task changed my working habit more than I expected. In a small standalone prototype, breaking something is cheap — you just fix it and move on. Inside an engine, even “small” edits feel heavier, because you can accidentally destabilize other subsystems. I started moving slower, testing more often, and treating changes as something that needs to be reversible.

4. Collision Response

The most time-consuming (and honestly the most interesting) work I did was collision response. Detection was already working — objects didn’t pass through each other — but the bounce behavior wasn’t convincing. The ball would reflect in ways that were hard to predict, and sometimes it just looked random.

Iteration 1 • Before Fix

The original behavior: collision detection worked (no tunneling), but the outgoing direction felt inconsistent and hard to predict.

4.1 Three Iterations

The first step was simply trying to remove the “chaos” and make behavior consistent. That helped, but the result was still weird: reflections became deterministic, yet often snapped into pure horizontal or vertical movement. It was cleaner, but it still didn’t match how your brain expects a bounce to work.

Eventually I moved to proper vector reflection using surface normals. Once that was in place, the difference was immediate: the ball reflected according to its angle of incidence, instead of snapping to axis-aligned shortcuts. I have videos for each stage (random-ish → structured but wrong → physically reasonable), and the progression is very clear.

Iteration 2 • Deterministic, Still Wrong

After the first improvement: bounce directions became consistent, but the reflection was still simplified (often snapping into pure horizontal/vertical motion).

4.2 Why “Almost Correct” Is Loud

What surprised me is how noticeable “almost correct” physics can be. A system can be deterministic and still feel wrong. And when it feels wrong, it also becomes harder to reason about and harder to debug, because you’re never sure whether the next weird bounce is a one-off or a symptom of the underlying rule.

Once reflection was based on a consistent mathematical rule, the whole system became easier to think about. It didn’t just look better — it became more predictable in a good way, the kind that helps you actually build on top of it.

Iteration 3 • Physically Reasonable

Final behavior: reflection follows the angle of incidence (vector reflection with surface normals), so trajectories feel coherent and predictable in the “right” way.

5.What Actually Felt Challenging

The hardest part wasn’t writing code. It was deciding where logic belongs. In a small prototype, it’s easy to mix everything together. In an engine, that becomes messy quickly.

With collision response, I kept asking myself: Is this engine logic? Is this game logic? Am I fixing the symptom or the structure?

I didn’t always get it right the first time. But I started noticing when something “felt wrong” architecturally, even if it worked. That’s probably the biggest shift for me so far.

6.Looking Back

None of these tasks were spectacular. They were small, technical, sometimes tedious. But they made me slower in a good way.

I now pay more attention to mathematical correctness, structural responsibility, and whether a quick fix is hiding a deeper issue.

If I restarted this work, I would test systems in isolation earlier and define boundaries more clearly. But overall, I’m glad I had to deal with things that were “almost correct.” Because those are harder to notice than things that completely fail. And fixing “almost correct” systems is where most of the learning happened.
