History as a Mechanic:
Respecting the Source

Reading time: 8 min

When we adapt a historical event into a board game, we walk a fine line between simulation and simplification. How do we gamify the Cuban Missile Crisis without trivializing the fear of nuclear annihilation?

The Card-Driven Revolution

Before Twilight Struggle, historical wargames were often bogged down by "chrome"—hundreds of specific rules for specific units. The genius of the Card-Driven Game (CDG) system is that it outsources historical flavor to the cards, leaving the core mechanics clean.

In Twilight Struggle, the event "NATO" isn't a complex rule about military alliances. It's simply a card that, when played, allows the US player to place influence in Europe more easily. The history is embedded in the effect, not the rulebook.

"The best historical games don't just teach you dates and names; they force you to feel the same pressures that the historical leaders felt."

Pax Pamir and the Loyalty Mechanic

Cole Wehrle's Pax Pamir takes this a step further. The game is about the Great Game in Afghanistan, but you don't play as the empires (Britain or Russia). You play as Afghan tribal leaders trying to manipulate these empires.

The brilliance lies in the "Loyalty" mechanic. Your loyalty is fluid. You might support the British one turn because they are strong, and switch to the Russians the next because the British have become too strong. This mechanic perfectly encapsulates the realpolitik of the era: survival over ideology.

Designing for Outcome vs. Process

A common trap in historical design is "scripting"—forcing the game to follow the actual history. If Germany must invade Poland on Turn 3, the players have no agency.

Instead, we should design for historical process. Create the economic and political incentives that led to the invasion of Poland, and see if the players choose to do it. If the mechanics accurately model the pressures of the time, history will often repeat itself naturally, without a script.

Conclusion

Gamifying history is an act of empathy. By stepping into the shoes of historical figures and wrestling with their constrained choices, players gain a deeper understanding than any textbook could provide. Our job as designers is to build the stage, not write the script.
