2. Game Description

2.1. Gameplay Patterns Overview

To enable the use of academic concepts when describing our games and to support the further analysis, we adopt Gameplay Design Patterns to standardize the terminology we will use. Throughout the rest of this paper, these patterns will be written in ALL CAPS for consistency.

Patterns defined by ourselves:

  • Tokens: A physical or digital piece that represents something in the game.
  • Card sequences: A set of adjacent number Cards.
  • Downtime: The time you spend waiting while other players take their turns.
  • Serpentine order: An order moves back and forth like a snake.
  • Fair round: Finish the round so all players have an equal number of turns.
  • Robbery: Players can prevent other players from acquiring resources or stealing resources from other players.

2.2. No Thanks!

No Thanks! is a minimalist push-your-luck card game built around a shared deck, an amount of tokens as RESOURCES, and A simple betting-based accept–reject mechanism.

At the Startgame, nine cards are removed Randomness, and each players get 11 tokens.

Players TAKE TURNS to play this game. First player revealing the top number-card and must either take it—an IRREVERSIBLE EVENT that adds its value to their scores or refuse it by using a token. The card you reject, along with the tokens accumulated on it, will be passed to the next player. This card will circulate among the players until someone takes it with all the tokens on it and then flips the next card.

When the deck is exhausted, the game over occurs and END STATE SCORING is triggered: Each player’s score is the sum of their number card minus their tokens, with Card sequences only counting the value of the smallest card. The player with the lowest scores wins in the end.

Figure 1: The MDA and pattern diagram of No thanks

2.3. Catan

This MULTIPLAYER GAME has players acting as pioneers focused on CONSTRUCTION, COLLECTION, and expansion. They build settlements, cities, and roads, choose COOPERATION or COMPETITION, and aim to reach 10 victory points to WIN BY ENDING GAMEPLAY. Players gain resources through DICE rolls, handle RANDOMNESS, practice RESOURCE MANAGEMENT, make AREA CONTROL decisions, engage in TRADING, and use or avoid ROBBERY to affect opponents.

Each turn begins with rolling two DICE to determine which Tiles produce Resources. Rolling a 7 activates the robber, blocking a TILES and allowing the player to hinder others. Otherwise, adjacent settlements gain 1 Resource and cities gain 2. The player may then initiate TRADE, exchanging Resources with the supplier at 4:1, negotiating any ratio with other players. Next, the player chooses whether to proceed with CONSTRUCTION. Settlements and cities increase income and points (cities require upgrading from settlements); roads expand building options but give no points; development Cards provide single-use abilities.

3. Player Engagement

3.1. No Thanks!

Downtime

No Thanks! maintains player engagement by betting and Tactical PLANNING with high decision density on minimal rules. Because turns are fast and the choice space is small but meaningful, players keep making decision with short Downtime.

token M → Resource Management D and NEGATIVE FEEDBACK LOOPS

As Figure 1 shows, the token system uses RESOURCE MANAGEMENT and RESOURCE CAPS to make every decision feel valuable. Each “No thanks” spends a limited number of tokens, turning refuse into a carefully weighted choice: on the one hand, the player can temporarily avoid taking a high-value penalty Card; on the other hand, they reduce their flexibility in future turns. As more tokens are placed on the same Card, that Card becomes increasingly valuable to players.

Figure 3: The feedback loop and game end condition in No Thanks !

This structure effectively creates a kind of NEGATIVE FEEDBACK LOOPS in the orange part of Figure 3 within the flow of play. When players have many tokens, they are more willing to refuse cards and reduce their token supply; at the same time, the more tokens accumulate on a Card, which means token in players' hands decrease, players tend to take it even its Scores value is high, because the tokens partly offset for the penalty. This tends to pull the token pile toward a Player Balance.

In players side, players who frequently use tokens to avoid risk are more likely to run out later and be forced to take suffocating Cards, and receive some tokens as compensation. In the short term, this restores part of their ability to choose, bringing the situation back to a relatively controllable range.

randomness

randomness and Uncertainty of Information information help sustain interest over repeated plays. Removing nine Cards from the deck at the start introduces UNCERTAINTY OF INFORMATION: perhaps you had planned everything well, but an unknown discarded Card could completely ruin your overall strategy.

3.2. Catan

Dice M → Randomness D → Luck A

The game has each player take turns rolling the Dice and the result does not affect only the player who rolls, it affects everyone. Based on the Dice as Mechanics, it leads to Randomness as Dynamics, and finally generate the feeling of Luck which is one kind of Aesthetics. As a result, all players must pay attention, keeping them consistently engaged. Because the outcome is purely random, players naturally hope for a result that benefits them. And even if it doesn’t, it doesn’t matter, the next random roll comes immediately, giving them a new chance.

Area Control

This is one of the most important decisions in the game. Players must choose which nodes to occupy and how to place their roads. To do this, they need to constantly watch how the Tiles change and whether other players are approaching the nodes they intend to claim. They must also consider how to build their own roads to reach those target nodes. This continuously shifting situation keeps players thinking and making decisions, which in turn sustains their engagement.

3.3. Comparison

This two game both introduce randomness to keep players exception to the match, but the way and the impact of randomness are different. In Catan, the main influence on the game state comes from Dice-driven internal randomness, which adds uncertainty but also reduces the weight of player decisions and creates a sense of losing control. The internal randomness in No Thanks! comes from the revealed Cards, which is generally fair and manageable for all players. The main randomness in No Thanks! is an external randomness - the nine discarded Cards, which are the biggest strategic uncertainty. This kind of randomness increases the amount of player interaction and also enhances Replayability. Catan also has external randomness — the placement of the starting map Tiles. This is a good design choice that increases Replayability without reducing strategic depth. The map setup variation in Gaia Project is similar to this.

Both games make use of RESOURCE MANAGEMENT, but the feedback structures are fundamentally different. In Catan, Resources primarily create a snowballing POSITIVE FEEDBACK LOOP that allows players to expand their advantage and move toward victory. In No Thanks!, tokens instead function as a kind of NEGATIVE FEEDBACK LOOPS: keeping too many tokens until the end is no longer valuable, but running out of tokens is also dangerous, so managing the count of token becomes crucial for players.

Like No Thanks!, in order to keep players constantly focused, most well-designed party games try to minimize Downtime to create a fast-paced and cheerful atmosphere, from simple as Scout to more complex as Heat: Pedal to the Metal. In contrast, games with higher strategic depth generally find this much harder to achieve. Besides, although Catan’s rules are not difficult, the actions are quite numerous, which often leads to very long downtime in beginner games and generate a very poor player experience.

4. Game Pace

4.1. No Thanks!

In No Thanks!, the game ends when the draw deck has been completely exhausted, as illustrated in the red part of Figure 3. If we treat the process from revealing a Card until that Card is finally taken by some player as one macro-turn, the game is effectively capped at 24 such turns. This places an overall cap on the game’s total duration.

Within each of these macro-turns, the amount of play that can happen is also tightly controlled. The current Card can only circulate around the table through a sequence of simple pass-or-take decisions, and this binary decision involves no additional multi-step action chains. So with the quickly decisions, downtime stays short, which fit with the fast-paced Principle of party games.

4.2. Catan

Winning by Ending Gameplay, Main Goal and Positive Feedback Loops

Players have clear goals and a clear win condition: reaching 10 Scores. As each match progresses, someone will eventually hit 10 points first, so the game reliably ends around the expected timeframe. But, having that goal alone isn’t enough. If players simply gained Resources at a constant rate, they would quickly get bored because the pace of change would be too uniform. However, as Figure 4 shows, the game builds a Positive Feedback Loop to resource production: the more buildings a player constructs, the faster they generate Resources, and the easier it becomes to construct even more buildings. As rounds progress, Construction accelerates, and the pace of change increases. This ensures the game won’t remain in a stable equilibrium; instead, it will escalate toward an increasingly unbalanced state over time, eventually producing a winner.

Figure 4: The Positive Feedback Loop of Catan

4.3. Comparison

No Thanks! uses a linear, fixed number of turns, controlling the game’s duration through a simplified binary decision structure. Love Letter and 6 nimmt! Plus also use a similar approach. Besides, Catan ends when someone achieves victory, so in theory the number of rounds in Catan can be unlimited. However, because of the positive feedback loops, the speed of Resource acquisition keeps accelerating, creating a converging pace that eventually brings the game to an end.

5. Player Interaction

5.1. No Thanks!

Player Unpredictability D → Tactical Planning D → End State Scoring D

In No Thanks!, all decisions are made in a fully shared GAME BOARD without a individual area. This common focus means that, when deciding whether to accept or refuse the Card, players must not only evaluate its face value, but also use the same public information to infer other players' risk tolerance and likely responses, creating a continuous psychological mind game, just as shown in Figure 1. In some situations, passing means potentially gaining one token from each opponent if the Card eventually returns, but there is also the risk that the Card you want might be taken by an opponent.

At the same time, the rule that Card sequence only score as their lowest value turns some card-taking decisions from merely “losing fewer points” into genuine structural optimizations of the entire Card set. An unattractive Card might immediately decrease one's expected Scores if it can be merged into an existing sequence. So players may sacrifice their own benefit to cut dowm someone else’s Card sequence to prevent them from gaining more advantage. However, in this lose-lose situation, the only ones who benefit are the other players who haven't participate in. Therefore, when you decide to break someone else’s sequence, you must also consider whether other players are willing to intervene, and whether the player holding the sequence might push their luck for one more round of tokens. In this intense psychological game, whether your decisions are correct depends entirely on your opponents’ choices. This pushes interaction to its extreme.

5.2. Catan

Resources M → Trading/Area Control D → Cooperation/Competition A

As Figure 2 shows, Resources are the basic Mechanics, and using trade and Area Control as Dynamics, the game generates cooperation and the feeling of Competition. Trading is one of the most engaging parts of the game. You must negotiate with other players and convince them to use Resources to trade with you. Players can also lie—making promises and then breaking them. This interaction forces players to think: Should I trade? Will this help the other player surpass me? These questions make the decision-making process fun, and the social interaction happens naturally in the thinking. On the other hand, about Area Control, because players cannot occupy the same node, and the spaces around a building cannot be occupied, COMPETITION naturally forms around valuable nodes. Players must rush to claim favorable positions while blocking others whenever possible. So, these dynamics works together to let players feel they are both in cooperation and COMPETITION.

Robbery

This is another highly entertaining system. When a player rolls a 7, they can move the robber to any tile to block its resource production and steal one Resource from a player adjacent to that tile. This makes players consider which Resources they can take advantage of and which Resources they should block to hinder others. Creates more direct COMPETITION between players.

5.3. Comparison

No Thanks! offers players a purely competitive form of interaction. Even when multiple players need to work together to stop someone who is about to win, this doesn’t create real cooperation—because each player is often unwilling to take on extra penalties just to block the leading player.

In Catan, although everyone is ultimately trying to win, trading creates opportunities for mutual benefit. Interaction is no longer just about hindering others; it can genuinely help both sides develop. As a result, player interaction in Catan is much more complex. Players must think about whether to trade, when to initiate a trade, and what each exchange means—not simply how to obstruct opponents.

6. A Sense of Achievement

6.1. No Thanks!

tactical planningD → Achievement A

In No Thanks!, completing a Card sequence can decrease a big amount of your Scores, but you must consider the risk that such a Card will be deliberately taken by an opponent or discarded in the startgame and never appear. Therefore, it's both difficult and getting closer to winning, and also provides a feeling of “correct” decision-making, which can provide a strong sense of achievement.

Meanwhile, as stated in 4.1, every decision is a psychological game involving everyone. Successfully judging this trade-off and “winning” these mind games also contributes to the player’s feeling of achievement.

Also, Luck will influence the sense of achievement. We will discuss it more in Comparison. All these relationships are shown in Figure 1.

6.2. Catan

Resources M → Resource Management D → Difficulty/Achievement A

As Figure 2 shows, the early game is intentionally difficult: players gather Resources and build at a very slow pace. However, with Resource Management and the Construction of buildings, once the Positive Feedback Loop starts operating, their resource production accelerates dramatically. This contrast between the slow beginning and the rapid mid-to-late-game progression gives players a sense of overcoming obstacles and experiencing growth.

ScoresM → Main Goal/Winning by Ending Gameplay D → Achievement A

One of the player’s main goals is simply to gain enough Scores, win against others. Starting from a fair beginning and achieving victory despite opponents’ interference—even with some luck involved—creates a strong sense of accomplishment. Winning also allows players to validate their ideas and strategies. Although a well-designed game shouldn’t have a single dominant solution, players are always fascinated by the search for an optimal strategy, and victory feels like proof that their approach worked. Because of this, winning delivers not only in-game satisfaction but also the meta-level feeling of “I solved this problem.”

Beyond the main win condition, the game provides two additional scoring paths: the longest road and the largest army (most knight Cards). Neither belongs to anyone at the start, but once the game reaches the mid-game, a player may claim one of these bonuses first. This makes COMPETITION more intense and turns these goals into meaningful strategic targets. Securing either bonus often brings a player close to victory, giving alternative playstyles their own moments of surprise and achievement. For designers, offering multiple victory routes—rather than a single fixed path—is crucial for maintaining variety and rewarding unconventional strategies.

6.3. Comparison

No Thanks! is a very fast-paced decision-making game, with each match ending quickly. In contrast, Catan requires players to manage Resources, build settlements, and make long-term strategic decisions—actions that naturally create a sense of achievement. Beyond this, Catan also encourages off-table thinking as players explore optimal strategies. Because of its higher complexity and deeper decision-making, players must consider multiple layers of information: how to use Resources, how to negotiate and compete with others, and how to claim key nodes on the map. As a result, winning in Catan feels far more rewarding. By comparison, much of the achievement in No Thanks! comes from Luck. The decision space is narrower, and Randomness has a greater influence on the outcome.

Player Balance also greatly affects players’ sense of accomplishment. If the outcome is caused by imbalance, the losing players may feel even more upset while their effort was wasted; and the winning players can’t fully enjoy a sense of achievement either, since it doesn’t feel like they won through their own skill. Going first in No Thanks! isn’t necessarily an advantage, and players’ decisions don’t differ much because of turn order, so the game tends to stay balanced overall. In Catan, although the serpentine order is used during the startgame to handle first player advantages, the winning by ending gameplay causes players earlier in turn get one extra action compared to the later, creating a severe imbalance. Many games solve this kind of imbalance through fair rounds, such as Splendor and Dune: Imperium.

7. Limitations of Models and Critical Reflections

When using patterns, we spent a lot of time trying to match our ideas with the patterns already on the list. But many mechanics can be explained in different ways, trying to draw strict lines between these vague actions can feel unnecessary. This level of detail is useful in academic writing, but when we use patterns for our own design or analysis, we don’t need to follow the pattern wiki so strictly.

However, it must be acknowledged that the analytical approach of patterns is highly effective, both in game design and game analysis. Moreover, even when two patterns are similar, there are definitional differences between them. Understanding why these differences exist helps us gain a deeper insight into game mechanics and the purposes for which certain patterns are used in games.

Additionally, patterns lack a hierarchical structure, so we must rely on frameworks like MDA or others to clarify the connections and influences between them, but the boundaries of the MDA framework are also very blurry. While thinking through this process helps us speculate about the designer's intentions behind certain patterns, the overall approach can still seem quite chaotic.

Furthermore, even if we deconstruct all the patterns within a game and reconstruct the structure as completely as possible, there is still no way to fully represent the player's decision-making process. How players think, how they make decisions, and what factors influence their decisions cannot be conveyed through patterns and MDA. Yet, this is a crucial part of the player experience and a key focus that designers should pay attention to.
